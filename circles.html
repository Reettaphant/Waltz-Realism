At the beginnign of each turn in a multipolar world, the computer checks if powers of states are going to update. Each state has a probability of their powers growing that is influenced by the intellectual and the territorial advantages of that state

Sometimes states powers are not updated. In a multipolar world, states may update their powers after a peaceful turn. How much the powers are updated is chosen at random, and sometimes no powers are updated. In a bipolar world powers are never updated. This is not because shifts in the state powers would not occur, but it is because these shifts do not affect the way in which these worlds function.

When the average power of the states gets too large, the powers of the states are scaled down so that the states can be better represented on screen. In defensive realism, only the relative powers of states, how weak or strong states are in relation to each other, not their absolute powers, is important. This is why scaling down state powers will not affect the way that the simulation is run

The assumptions made in this simulation is that an alliance will only attack another alliance if that alliance's attack power is greater than the defending power of another alliance. The simulation also makes an assumption, often made by defensive realists, that the attacking alliance is always slightly disadvantaged. Taking this into account, the simulation equates an alliance's defensive powers the power of that alliance, but the attacking power of the same alliance is only 90% of their attacking power. Thus, if the powers of all alliances are the same, or very close to being the same, no alliance will consider it in their interests to attack another alliance.

According to Waltz's defensive realism, bipolar systems are supposed to be durable. This bipolar system has, however, disintegrated for no apparent reason. A similar situation was seen at the end of the cold war, when Waltz's realism failed to predict the collapse of the USSR. Waltz does not think that this is a weakness in his theory. He argues that the purpose of the theory is to identify patterns, but not predict particular outcomes. Waltz thus argues that his job is not to explain the disintegration of particular bipolar systems, and the reason for the collapse of the bipolar order in this simulation shall remain a mystery.

Unipolar systems are durable, but they do not last forever. In this simulation, due to the unipolar power overstreching itself, after the first three unipolar turns, the hegemon's powers will start to decline.
